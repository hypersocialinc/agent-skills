# Diligence readiness — don't let a preventable problem kill the deal

Most deals that die in diligence die of self-inflicted, preventable wounds — disorganized or missing documentation is cited as a primary factor in a large share of failed deals. The fix is cheap and almost entirely front-loadable. Get this in order *before* you're in a process, not under deadline pressure mid-deal.

## The #1 deal-killer: IP assignment gaps

If any founder, employee, or contractor who built core technology never signed an agreement assigning that work to the company, **the company may not legally own its own product.** This surfaces in legal diligence and can tank or delay a deal outright.

- Confirm **every** founder, employee, and contractor has a signed IP/invention-assignment agreement.
- Chase down early contractors, agencies, and pre-incorporation work especially.
- Fix gaps *now* — retroactive assignments are far easier before a buyer is looking.

## Clean cap table

An inaccurate cap table causes disputes, delays, and derailed deals — and it's the first thing a buyer reconciles.

- Cap table reconciles to issued equity, option grants, SAFEs/notes, and board consents.
- Every change (new grants, departures, conversions) is documented and reflected.
- Liquidation preferences and any side letters are accurately captured (this also feeds the structure analysis in `deal-structures.md`).

## The data room

- Have it **ready early** (ideally before you're in active talks) — building under pressure produces wrong versions and gaps.
- Cover the standard set: incorporation docs, cap table, financials (actuals + model), IP assignments, key contracts, material agreements, employee/option docs, product/roadmap, key metrics.
- **Curate, don't dump.** Too many documents buries the ones that matter and slows the buyer; include what's relevant and well-organized.
- **Review everything before granting access** — every doc must be accurate and consistent with what you've represented. A contradiction between the data room and your reps is a credibility (and indemnity) problem.

## Other common surprises to pre-empt

- Unsigned/again-unassigned consultant work, open-source license obligations in the codebase.
- Missing board/stockholder consents for past issuances.
- Misclassified contractors vs. employees.
- Outstanding promises (verbal equity, side deals) not on the cap table.

## Quick checklist

- [ ] Every founder/employee/contractor has signed IP assignment?
- [ ] Cap table reconciled and current, with prefs/side letters captured?
- [ ] Data room built, curated (not dumped), and reviewed for accuracy before access?
- [ ] Open-source/license obligations and contractor classifications checked?
- [ ] No off-cap-table equity promises lurking?
