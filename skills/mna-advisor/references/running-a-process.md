# Running a process — leverage, sequencing, posture

Reference for step 5 (run a process) and step 6 (control information and posture).

## A process beats an event

A single buyer setting the terms is not a negotiation — it's an order-taker relationship. The founder's leverage comes almost entirely from the *credible* existence of an alternative. Build one before you need it.

- **Develop a real BATNA / second party** even if the first is going well. Warm is enough; it doesn't need to be a signed offer to change the founder's posture.
- **Never single-thread.** One champion who's on leave, exiting, or lukewarm is a fragile deal. One buyer is a price-taker's position. Cultivate multiple internal sponsors and, where possible, multiple acquirers.
- **Competitive tension is the lever** — you rarely have to *name* the other party (often you must not). The founder's calm willingness to walk, and their timeline, do the work.

## Sequencing

Match the conversation to the deal's actual stage. Common ordering for a strategic acquisition:

1. **Product/strategic fit** — does the acquirer believe this is real and that it matters to them?
2. **Technical/diligence fit** — engineers kicking the tires. This is *diligence*, a step *toward* a deal, not evidence against one.
3. **Structure / price** — only when BD / corpdev enters. Don't pitch "acquire my company" to a room of engineers; win them on "it's real, it integrates, it scales," and let structure come when the deal owners arrive.

**Conviction and stage are separate variables.** A President-level champion who's excited but hasn't taken it to the deal team = high conviction, early stage. Don't collapse them into one read in either direction.

## Posture — hold vs. lean in

- **Hold** (don't volunteer numbers, take your time) when you have **runway** and the counterparty needs progress. Holding gracefully — without threats — inverts pressure toward the party with the deadline.
- **Lean in** (enthusiasm, speed, responsiveness) when you're **winning a fit assessment** or building a champion. Eagerness *is* the right move there; a holding posture would read as disinterest.
- Read which mode each track is in *independently*. The same founder may be holding with one acquirer and leaning into another the same week.
- **Anchor shape before number.** Lead with the structure ("small acquisition + 2-year retention; asset price just clears investor consent; real economics in the retention bucket"), then the range. It frames the number before it lands.
- **Use modesty as a trust signal — but only where the culture rewards it.** In trust-based / band-driven cultures, "I'm not here to squeeze you" can land better than a hardball anchor and still reach a strong number via the right bucket. With a generic strategic or a transactional buyer, stay **collaborative but not submissive** — modesty there just leaves money on the table. Read the culture first (see below).

## Information control

- **Never cross-name competing acquirers.** Competitive context changes *posture*, not talking points. Naming names leaks leverage and can poison a culture-sensitive process.
- Decide deliberately who knows what, and write the guardrails into the deal's `CLAUDE.md` so every session respects them.
- High-stakes messages: get **one outside reader** before sending. Outside perspective beats another round of internal iteration.

## Reading the acquirer's culture

- **Band / fairness-driven** (e.g., trust-based cultures): hold salary in-band, put premium in M&A bucket, submissive/collaborative tone, frame any BATNA as *timeline pressure* not threat.
- **Acqui-hire-fluent / green-field**: the acquirer who *lacks* the capability and knows it offers cleaner logic than one who'd "just rebuild it." Green-field need = higher willingness to pay + faster conviction.
- Match the channel: some cultures negotiate through the product sponsor, others through corpdev. Confirm the channel early; using the wrong one stalls or annoys.

## Triangulation

Pull from: **counsel** (consent, IP transferability, carve-out mechanics), **tax advisor** (bucket treatment, QSBS), **board / preferred holders** (consent readiness), **operators who've done the same deal** (precedents, channel, realistic ranges). Then the founder decides — advisors inform, they don't drive.
